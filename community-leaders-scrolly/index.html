Community Challenges in Psychedelic Safety

Survey Results from 40 Community and Student Leaders

Across the globe, psychedelic societies and student organizations are witnessing a shift. As interest in psychedelics increases, so do the risks. PSI surveyed 35 leaders from psychedelic societies and 5 representatives from various Students for Sensible Drug Policy chapters worldwide to understand the safety challenges their communities face daily.

Safety as the Foundation

100% of respondents identified safety as critical to their communities.

Over 8 million Americans used psychedelics in 2023, while clinical trials typically enroll only hundreds to thousands of participants — meaning the vast majority of psychedelic use happens outside clinical and regulated settings. This is where community organizations play a crucial role.

Community leaders understand something fundamental: safety isn't just about crisis prevention. It's about creating the conditions where people can explore consciousness, process trauma, and experience healing without unnecessary risk. When asked about their communities' values and priorities, leaders revealed a sophisticated understanding of how safety enables transformation.

One leader explained what this means in practice:

"I've been in a lot of spaces where set and setting were not considered. (...) Fostering a space of safety with education first and foremost allows for a deeper experience in a safe container."

This isn't just about preventing bad trips—it's about creating safe conditions for profound transformation, where education and knowledge form the foundation for healing, growth, and lasting change.

The priorities they identified:

Emphasize education and knowledge sharing 100%
See safety as enabling personal and collective healing 100%
Believe in community responsibility and ethics 100%
Focus on ensuring access and equity 95%

Causes of Psychedelic Harm

When 100% of respondents say safety is critical, they're speaking from experience. These communities have witnessed harm firsthand—they've seen what happens when education is missing, when practitioners lack training, when systems fail, and when vulnerable people fall through the cracks. Understanding where safety breaks down is essential to building it back up. Community leaders identified four interconnected causes of harm, each affecting communities in distinct but overlapping ways.

The Knowledge Gap (100% of communities affected)

  • Treating psychedelics as quick fixes
  • Misinformation spreading faster than education
  • First-timers going in completely unprepared
"My biggest concern is just really like level setting with people that this isn't just like an easy button that you push. This isn't just like another pharmaceutical drug that you can just take."

Unsafe Practitioners (100% report concerns)

  • Self-proclaimed facilitators without training
  • Facilitators crossing boundaries
  • Culturally inappropriate care
"There is no way to really know about the ethics and reputation of the person leading the experience in an objective way. There are issues of trust. How do we establish trust BEFORE first contact?"

System Failures (83% experiencing barriers)

  • Criminalization preventing people from seeking help
  • No accountability systems for bad actors
  • Underground markets without labelling and testing standards
"There is no recourse for reporting of abuse in a criminalized system where the abuse itself is criminal yet dismissed because it took place 'under ground'."

Vulnerable Populations at Risk (90% worried)

  • Neurodivergent individuals without adapted support
  • Trauma survivors being retraumatized
  • Marginalized communities facing additional barriers
"A facilitator may not have great training or may take advantage of someone in a vulnerable state."

Safety Challenges Across Different Settings

Our research identified distinct safety challenges that emerge in five critical contexts:

At Festivals & Parties (especially reported by students)

  • Contaminated substances (56% report as top concern)
  • No quiet spaces for overwhelm
  • Peer pressure and suggestibility
  • Absent harm reduction services
"Ensuring drugs are not laced. We strongly encourage testing before ingesting."

When Taking Psychedelics Alone

  • No support during crisis (65% cite as major risk)
  • Panic and psychological overwhelm
  • Physical safety risks
  • Inability to call for help
"Risks of taking alone include overwhelm, feeling untethered—like they will never be normal or come back from this place."

In Small Groups

  • Group emotional responses
  • Pressure to dose higher
  • Lack of clear boundaries
  • No trained crisis support
"Is someone sober to help? Where is it being held? What is the experience and education of the facilitator?"

In Facilitated Sessions

  • Power abuse and boundary violations (70% say is a concern)
  • Inadequate screening for trauma/medications
  • Sexual misconduct
  • No integration support
"It took me 3 years to find a facilitator for me. Power, ego—calling themselves a 'Shaman' or 'Guru', thinking they can 'fix' or 'cure' people."

In Traditional/Spiritual Contexts

  • Cultural appropriation
  • Spiritual manipulation
  • Unsafe ceremonial practices
  • Trauma reactivation without support
"How is reciprocity being done? What is the ownership structure of retreats when claims are being made about following indigenous traditions and practice?"
