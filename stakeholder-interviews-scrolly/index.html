Part One

Understanding the Landscape

Psychedelic Safety Has Human Impacts

Nearly all stakeholders agree that psychedelics are powerful substances requiring careful handling, and how we approach safety now will determine whether psychedelics fulfill their therapeutic potential or set the field back.

Stakeholders see interconnected challenges: preventing harms like psychological trauma (82%), addressing systemic gaps like unethical practitioners (61%), and building foundational infrastructure (education, standards, accountability) that largely don't yet exist.

82% Reducing Harms
61% Systemic Gaps
96% Building Infrastructure

Where Things Could Go Wrong

Without proper safety measures, stakeholders envision a domino effect: increased harm leads to negative media coverage, which triggers policy reversals and ultimately prevents psychedelics from reaching those who need them most.

The fear of irreversible damage to public trust weighs heavily—once lost, that trust may never return. This matters because trust is the foundation everything else rests on—research funding, policy reform, therapeutic access, public acceptance.

Part Two

The Challenges We Face

The Coordination Challenge

A striking finding emerges when stakeholders identify major obstacles to progress: many point to internal field dynamics and coordination challenges.

Organizations work in silos, duplicating efforts rather than coordinating. Academic researchers face pressure to publish first rather than share data openly. Commercial interests sometimes prioritize profit over collaboration. And practitioners themselves struggle with ego and blind spots that can compromise safety. What's needed, stakeholders suggest, is a field-wide commitment to greater collaboration, maturity, and humility.

"It's still so fragmented, in terms of who has what information."
"I worry about duplication... I often feel frustrated by how many resources are going toward processes that already exist. It's the competition thing again."
"People on opposing sides, arguing about who's right, or who's wrong. Instead of a dialogue that increases opportunities to be curious together."
84% cite siloed operations as major barrier
Practitioner ego and competition as obstacles 60%
Field immaturity as challenge 53%
"Psychedelic exceptionalism" limiting learning 44%
Commercial competition overriding collaboration 42%

The field hasn't yet developed the collaborative infrastructure needed to pool knowledge, coordinate efforts, and work toward shared safety goals. Whether other goals become achievable may depend significantly on addressing these internal coordination challenges.

The Professional Crisis: Who's Qualified?

The field faces a fundamental challenge: we're training people to guide others through some of the most vulnerable moments of their lives, yet we lack agreement on what competence looks like.

Some programs offer weekend certifications while others require years of study. And when practitioners cause harm? There's minimal accountability infrastructure—very few registries, complaint systems, or ways to track or remove bad actors.

95% prioritize standardized, comprehensive training

The Problem

  • 74% report inconsistent training quality
  • 60% cite lack of accountability systems
  • 66% worry training organizations prioritize profit over education
  • 52% fear proliferation of unethical practitioners

What's Needed

  • 95% support standardized training
  • 74% emphasize ethics-focused curricula
  • 47% worry practitioners lack skills for adverse events
  • 70% say adverse events require specific protocols

The Emergency Response Gap

A critical vulnerability emerges in how existing emergency systems handle psychedelic crises. Respondents agree that first responders need training, yet minimal action has been made to provide it.

This gap means someone experiencing a difficult psychedelic experience might encounter emergency personnel who inadvertently escalate the situation through inappropriate interventions, turning a manageable situation into a traumatic one.

"It's a failing of our society that... the staff and the doctors and the nurses and so forth don't know what they should in many cases to be able to properly provide care in this situation."
"[A psychedelic crisis is] probably very different than many other types of trauma or emergency medicine situations that they encounter."

The Integration Crisis

Every practitioner surveyed agrees that comprehensive integration support is essential for safety—helping people process and incorporate their experiences. Seventy percent of stakeholders prioritize integration support networks, and 84% say thorough preparation is critical. Yet clinical trials rarely provide ongoing or comprehensive support, leaving participants to navigate profound experiences alone. Moreover, 78% want research participants to be connected with external post-trial resources (such as community groups, therapists, or crisis lines), but most trials end without providing these connections.

Here's the economic reality: providing months of ongoing integration care on top of already expensive psychedelic sessions is very costly. This creates pressure to cut corners—terminating support too early, or skipping integration altogether to keep costs down.

Underground Models Show a Path Forward

Underground communities have developed cost-effective, peer-based integration models that offer ongoing support—not just the limited sessions typical of clinical trials. These grassroots organizations demonstrate what's possible when accessibility and community are prioritized. Stakeholders specifically mentioned these organizations as examples of effective peer-based integration models:

  • Peer-led by people with lived experience
  • Flexible formats—online, phone, text, or in-person

The Equity and Diversity Challenge

Stakeholders agree that psychedelic healing should not be reserved for only those who can afford it. Yet marginalized communities face compounded barriers at every level.

Historical medical exclusion has created deep mistrust in marginalized communities. Cultural frameworks get ignored or appropriated, legal risks hit harder for those already surveilled, and cost puts access out of reach for many. These barriers extend into clinical research itself, where the very people who could benefit most are systematically excluded from the studies that shape treatment approaches.

The Underground Reality

Stakeholders acknowledge that unregulated use will continue regardless of legal status. Rather than ignoring this reality, the underground ecosystem is already building sophisticated safety infrastructure.

Beyond basic harm reduction, underground communities are engaging in collective ethical inquiry—examining their own practices, discussing boundaries and consent, and identifying where harms occur. They're also establishing peer accountability systems, including efforts like the Shine Collective, which tracks practitioner complaints and helps communities identify concerning patterns. These organic efforts represent a critical advancement: communities developing safety systems without waiting for legal frameworks or institutional approval.

Believe legal frameworks must reduce reliance on unregulated settings 95%
Support making harm reduction training accessible to underground practitioners 90%
Report peer support networks forming organically 68%
Support voluntary ethical certification 64%
Researching ethical challenges in unregulated settings 61%

The Need for Data-Driven Solutions

The psychedelic field faces a fundamental data problem. Enormous resources flow toward understanding safety in highly controlled clinical contexts, while the vast majority of use happens in real-world settings those trials tell us little about.

85% recognize trial results don't translate to real-world safety

The most fundamental gap: we lack consensus on what we're measuring. What does "safety" actually mean in different contexts? What distinguishes an adverse event from a therapeutically challenging experience? Without agreed-upon definitions, different researchers, practitioners, and programs can't compare their findings or learn from each other's experiences.

Beyond definitions, basic questions remain unanswered: How often do adverse events really occur? Which populations face higher risks? How do medications and existing conditions interact with psychedelics? What happens in the months and years after use? The absence of systematic data collection means every practitioner, user, and policymaker is making decisions without sufficient evidence.

"We're not collecting enough real-world data about people as they are... I think we actually know pretty very little about who is using psychedelics in the wild, why they're using, what's happening to them when they use."
"[We need to] have a better definition of adverse events and a system for collecting them."
"Uniform data, and that data being transparent and that data being shared. Right now, it feels like there's not really a true system of really seeing what's going on through the numbers."

Part Three

The Path Forward: Building Solutions

Building Solutions: Three Pillars of Safety

Where stakeholders find consensus, a clear architecture for safety emerges. Education ensures people know what they're getting into—from the public understanding risks before first use, to practitioners developing the competencies needed to care for vulnerable people safely. Standards create accountability and consistency—from consensus definitions of what "safety" and "adverse events" mean to who's qualified to practice, what protocols to follow when things go wrong, and how to track outcomes systematically. Equitable access ensures safety for everyone and builds pathways for marginalized communities to access psychedelics.

All three are necessary. Education alone can't prevent harm without standards to guide practice. Standards mean nothing without education to implement them. And neither matters if only some people can access them.
