Part 1

A Note on This Research

Colorado legalized psychedelic services in 2022, but we still have limited public data about what's happening on the ground. This research offers early signals from stakeholders navigating this emerging market—not definitive conclusions.

We surveyed 83 people across four stakeholder groups between 2024-2025 to understand who's involved, what challenges they face, and how the market is developing.

How We Did It

We combined ecosystem mapping with targeted surveys. First, we mapped Colorado's psychedelic landscape through publicly available sources, building a database of practitioners, centers, and supporting organizations. Then we surveyed stakeholders across these groups to understand their experiences, challenges, and perspectives.

Research Limitations

Small sample size. With only 83 respondents across all groups, we cannot make confident claims about Colorado's entire psychedelic market. These numbers provide early directional signals, not statistical certainty.

Selection bias. Our sample likely over-represents people connected to psychedelic advocacy networks and engaged stakeholders. We're missing voices from people who gave up trying to access services, providers who closed their practices, and those who never entered the system.

No demographic data. We don't know how well our sample represents Colorado's actual population by race, income, geography, age, or other demographic factors. This limits our ability to understand equity and access issues.

Self-reported data. All responses are self-reported perceptions and experiences, not verified through independent observation or administrative data.

Despite these constraints, clear patterns emerged that suggest structural challenges worth investigating. What follows are early signals, not final conclusions—but in a field with so little public data, even preliminary findings can help us ask better questions.

What These Findings Suggest

These aren't conclusions—they're patterns worth investigating further.

Three interconnected patterns emerged from this research that tell a story about Colorado's psychedelic ecosystem:

Together, these patterns reveal a fundamental tension: Colorado designed a system to ensure safety through regulation, but that system created economic barriers that exclude most of the people it was meant to serve.

1

The Affordability Trap

The Affordability Crisis Is Real

The gap between consumer affordability ($306-$706) and market prices ($1,200-$6,000) isn't just a mismatch—it's a structural incompatibility that defines who can access legal services.

59% of consumers can only afford under $1,000

100% of those choosing underground cite PRICE as a factor

78% need insurance, sliding scale, or payment plans

What this means: Without subsidies or significant cost reductions, licensed services will remain inaccessible to most Coloradans.

Licensing Costs Block Formalization

The affordability crisis extends to providers themselves. To become licensed, practitioners face $26K-$56K in upfront costs—which equals 13-28 months of gross revenue for the 50% of practitioners earning $1-3K/month from psychedelic services.

What this means: The system may inadvertently filter out the practitioners most committed to serving lower-income communities, as those with existing wealth are better positioned to absorb these costs.

This creates a vicious cycle: High regulatory costs force providers to charge high prices, which pushes consumers to underground options, which reduces the customer base for legal services, which makes it even harder for providers to achieve financial sustainability. The math simply doesn't work.

2

How People Respond

Trust Networks Matter More Than Regulation

83.3% of consumers rely on word-of-mouth recommendations to find services. Personal connection, cultural fit, and practitioner experience drive selection decisions far more than regulatory status.

What this means: Formal licensing alone won't determine consumer choices. Trust networks built through community relationships are more influential than government approval.

The Underground Isn't Going Away

People choose underground services not just for affordability, but for flexibility, cultural alignment, and established trust networks that licensed services don't necessarily replicate. For many, this isn't a compromise—it's a preference.

What this means: Even with more affordable licensed options, many will continue to choose underground providers based on trust, cultural fit, and service model preferences.

These aren't bugs in the system—they're adaptive responses. When formal pathways are unaffordable or inaccessible, people create informal ones. The question isn't whether these networks will exist, but how policy can work with them rather than against them.

3

The Deeper Systems at Play

Information Access Is an Equity Issue

87.5% of consumers report not knowing how to access psychedelic services. Those without connections to advocacy networks or personal referrals are effectively locked out, even if they could afford services.

What this means: The word-of-mouth discovery system—while trusted—reinforces existing privilege. If you don't already know someone in the network, finding services is nearly impossible.

Reciprocity Needs Deeper Work

While 73% of practitioners are engaged with or exploring reciprocity practices and 75% want to learn more, only 17% share Indigenous context extensively with clients. More critically, Indigenous perspectives on these efforts are largely missing from the conversation.

What this means: Interest doesn't equal right relationship. Without Indigenous voices at the center of these conversations, reciprocity risks becoming performative rather than transformative.

These patterns don't just coexist—they reinforce each other. Limited information access keeps services hidden from those who need them most. Reliance on word-of-mouth networks concentrates access among those already connected. And without Indigenous voices centered in reciprocity work, the ecosystem risks replicating the same extractive dynamics it claims to move beyond.

Where Things Stand

Progressing with Cautious Optimism

Despite these challenges, stakeholders rate Colorado's implementation at 6.5/10, with 42.9% clustering around "cautious optimism." There's broad consensus: steady progress is happening, and there's significant room for improvement.

What this means: The system is functional and progressing, but not yet meeting its full potential to serve the community equitably. The infrastructure is being built—the question is whether it will evolve to address these foundational tensions.

What We Don't Know

This research raises as many questions as it answers. Here's what's still missing:

Missing Voices

  • People who gave up: We didn't capture people who tried to access services but stopped due to cost, complexity, or lack of information
  • Demographic representation: We don't know how well our sample represents Colorado's actual population by race, income, geography, or other factors
  • Indigenous perspectives: While we asked about reciprocity practices, we didn't survey Indigenous communities about their perspectives on the program
  • Failed providers: Practitioners and centers that tried to operate but closed aren't represented here

Missing Data

  • Actual client outcomes: We have what centers report, but no standardized outcome tracking across providers
  • Adverse events: No systematic reporting of harms, emergencies, or negative experiences in either licensed or underground settings
  • Market size: We don't know how many people are actually using psychedelic services in Colorado (licensed or underground)
  • Provider sustainability: No longitudinal data on whether practitioners and centers can remain financially viable
  • Geographic distribution: We don't know where services are concentrated or what areas are underserved

Unanswered Questions

  • • Can licensed services ever reach the price point that most consumers can afford?
  • • What happens to underground practitioners as enforcement evolves?
  • • Will insurance ever cover psilocybin services? If so, when?
  • • Are there models from other states or countries that could address the affordability gap?
  • • How do we balance safety oversight with cultural/spiritual practices?

Why This Matters:

These gaps aren't just academic. They represent blind spots in how Colorado is building this ecosystem.

Without better data, we're making policy decisions in the dark.
