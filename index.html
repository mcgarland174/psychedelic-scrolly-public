What's Actually Happening in Colorado's Psychedelic Market?

Field Notes from 83 Early Conversations

Colorado legalized psychedelic services in 2022. But almost no one is tracking what's happening on the ground.

We surveyed 83 people across the ecosystem: 18 consumers, 12 individual practitioners, 23 organizations, and 26 ecosystem groups.

These aren't definitive answers—but they reveal patterns worth investigating.

A Note on This Research

⚠️

Research Limitations

Small sample (n=83) with significant limitations. Please read to understand why these are early signals, not conclusions.

This is exploratory work with significant limitations:

📊 Sample Limitations

  • Only 83 respondents total:
  • • 18 consumers | 12 practitioners | 23 organizations | 26 ecosystem groups
  • Likely over-represents people connected to psychedelic advocacy networks
  • Missing voices: people who gave up trying to access services, providers who quit, entire demographic groups

❓ What This Means

We cannot make confident claims about Colorado's entire market. These findings suggest patterns that deserve deeper investigation—not conclusions.

Despite these limitations, clear patterns emerged—patterns that suggest structural challenges in how Colorado's market is developing.

What follows are early signals that warrant closer investigation, not final conclusions. But in a field with so little public data, even preliminary findings can help us ask better questions.

The Math Problem:

How do you invest $12-20K to enter a field where half the practitioners make $1-3K/month?

Why This Matters: High-quality care exists in the underground market—but practitioners cannot sustain it long-term at prices clients can afford.

The question isn't about quality standards. It's about economic viability.

Can practitioners maintain professional-level care while earning $1-3K/month?

Can clients access professional-level care if practitioners need to charge more to survive?

This tension drives many practitioners to stay underground rather than pursue licensing.

What These Findings Suggest

These aren't conclusions—they're patterns worth investigating further.

1. The Affordability Crisis Is Real

The gap between consumer affordability ($306-$706 sweet spot) and market prices ($1,200-$6,000) isn't a small mismatch. It's a structural incompatibility.

Implication: Without insurance coverage or significant subsidies, licensed services will remain inaccessible to most Coloradans.

2. The Underground Isn't Going Away

People choose underground services not just because they're cheaper, but because they offer flexibility, cultural alignment, and trust networks that licensed services don't replicate.

Implication: Even if licensed services become more affordable, a significant portion of the market may continue to operate underground by choice.

3. Licensing Costs Create a Barrier to Formalization

At $12-20K to become licensed and 6-10 months of gross revenue for practitioners earning $1-3K/month, the barrier to entry is prohibitively high.

Implication: The current system may be filtering out exactly the practitioners who are most committed to serving lower-income communities.

4. Information Access Is an Equity Issue

If 83.3% of people find services through personal networks or advocacy groups, those without existing connections are locked out.

Implication: The current "word-of-mouth" discovery system reinforces existing privilege and limits broader access.

5. Reciprocity Interest Exists, But Implementation Needs Work

While 73% of providers are engaged or exploring reciprocity, we lack Indigenous community perspectives on whether these efforts are meaningful.

Implication: Interest is not the same as right relationship. The ecosystem needs Indigenous voices at the center of these conversations.

The Core Tension:

Colorado designed a system to ensure safety and quality through regulation. But that system has created economic barriers that exclude most of the people it was meant to serve.

The question isn't whether regulation matters. It's whether the current regulatory model can ever be equitable.

What We Don't Know

Research Gaps & Unanswered Questions

Missing voices, missing data, and unanswered questions that deserve deeper investigation. Click to expand.

▼

This research raises as many questions as it answers. Here's what's still missing:

🔍 Missing Voices

  • People who gave up: We didn't capture people who tried to access services but stopped due to cost, complexity, or lack of information
  • Demographic representation: We don't know how well our sample represents Colorado's actual population by race, income, geography, or other factors
  • Indigenous perspectives: While we asked about reciprocity practices, we didn't survey Indigenous communities about their perspectives on the program
  • Failed providers: Practitioners and centers that tried to operate but closed aren't represented here

📊 Missing Data

  • Actual client outcomes: We have what centers report, but no standardized outcome tracking across providers
  • Adverse events: No systematic reporting of harms, emergencies, or negative experiences in either licensed or underground settings
  • Market size: We don't know how many people are actually using psychedelic services in Colorado (licensed or underground)
  • Provider sustainability: No longitudinal data on whether practitioners and centers can remain financially viable
  • Geographic distribution: We don't know where services are concentrated or what areas are underserved

🤔 Unanswered Questions

  • • Can licensed services ever reach the price point that most consumers can afford?
  • • What happens to underground practitioners as enforcement evolves?
  • • Will insurance ever cover psilocybin services? If so, when?
  • • Are there models from other states or countries that could address the affordability gap?
  • • How do we balance safety oversight with cultural/spiritual practices?

Why This Matters:

These gaps aren't just academic. They represent blind spots in how Colorado is building this ecosystem.

Without better data, we're making policy decisions in the dark.

What Happens Next?

This research is a starting point. Here's what needs to happen to build on it:

For Policymakers & Regulators

  • → Track affordability and access data systematically
  • → Explore tiered licensing models that reduce barriers for community-based providers
  • → Consider insurance pathways or state-funded access programs
  • → Center Indigenous voices in reciprocity and program design

For Researchers

  • → Conduct larger-scale surveys with more diverse demographic representation
  • → Track client outcomes longitudinally across licensed and underground settings
  • → Study adverse events and harms systematically
  • → Map geographic distribution of services and access barriers

For Practitioners & Organizations

  • → Share data transparently about pricing, outcomes, and accessibility
  • → Collaborate across licensed/underground divide to learn from each other
  • → Develop sliding-scale and community-based funding models
  • → Engage meaningfully with Indigenous reciprocity, not performatively

For Funders

  • → Support access programs and sliding-scale services, not just infrastructure
  • → Fund independent research on equity and access barriers
  • → Prioritize Indigenous-led initiatives and reciprocity programs
  • → Invest in training scholarships for practitioners from underserved communities

Colorado has an opportunity to build something better.

But it requires honest conversations about who the current system serves—and who it leaves behind.
