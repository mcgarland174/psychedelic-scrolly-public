Understanding Product Safety Challenges

Survey Results from Hard-to-Access Supply Networks

PSI surveyed 20 underground manufacturers, which revealed significant concerns about product safety and authenticity in the psychedelic marketplace.

Thank you to PsiloSafe for their collaboration and support in collecting these responses.

Understanding the Landscape: Underground vs. Regulated

Underground Manufacturers

Operating in the gray market—community or ceremonial spaces outside formal regulatory frameworks. Similarly, underground therapeutic practitioners offering one-on-one sessions exist in this same gray area, providing individualized support without formal regulatory oversight.

Regulated Manufacturers

Operating in jurisdictions where psychedelics are legal for medical or therapeutic use, following state regulations on manufacturing, testing, and labeling.

What Underground Manufacturers Are Seeing

Product Integrity Concerns

85% of manufacturers identified authenticity issues in the market:

Inconsistent dosing across various products 94%
Competitors using false or misleading labels 76%
Products with undisclosed adulterants 30%
"Undisclosed and dangerous additives in products. Falsely disclosed products (saying Amanita is an ingredient but no amanita present etc)."
— Underground manufacturer

Impact on Consumers and Communities

These product integrity issues have real consequences. Manufacturers report witnessing concerning outcomes in their communities:

See direct safety risks to consumers 61%
Observe mis-dosing incidents in their communities 61%

Solutions from Within the Industry: Manufacturers' Recommendations

Those working in the underground market have clear ideas about what would improve safety:

Product Standards

  • → Standardized testing protocols (67%)
  • → Accurate labeling requirements (53%)
  • → Manufacturing quality controls (33%)

Accountability Systems

  • → Third-party certification (50%)
  • → Industry self-regulation (50%)
  • → Legalization essential for addressing safety (38%)
"Regulations on proper labeling procedures. Forums, articles and blogs to warn and educate people."
— Manufacturer recommendation

The Education Gap: What's Missing from the Market

75% of manufacturers identify critical gaps in consumer education

Essential Information Often Missing:

Limited information on set and setting
Absence of proper dosing guidance
Lack of informed consent practices (80%)
Missing harm reduction resources

Manufacturers recognize that even well-intentioned producers often fail to provide adequate educational materials with their products.

How Manufacturers Want to Address This

86% believe comprehensive consumer education is essential to improving safety:

  • Creation of forums, guides, and safety resources
  • Integration of harm reduction principles
  • Clear communication about preparation and risk factors

The Bigger Picture: A Shared Vision for Safety

Manufacturers emphasize the need for comprehensive approaches:

Call for evidence-based practices over marketing hype 92%

Support third-party testing certification 53%

Safety requires the intersection of:

Education
Harm Reduction

Challenges and Opportunities: What Stands in the Way

Manufacturers identify key obstacles:

Structural Barriers

  • Lack of regulatory framework makes standardization difficult
  • No unified certifying body for establishing best practices and maintaining accountability
  • Need for confidential certification given legal risks

Cultural Barriers

  • Fear of legal repercussions prevents open dialogue
  • Bad actors undermining responsible manufacturers

Manufacturers are clear about what's missing:

"Certifying body who can keep confidentiality while reviewing our claims on our packaging."
— Underground manufacturer

Building a Responsible Industry: A Collective Responsibility

The manufacturers surveyed aren't just identifying problems—they're actively seeking solutions. Their message is clear: safety improvements require industry-led standards and testing protocols now, with self-regulation filling the gap until legalization enables formal oversight and accountability.

Key Takeaways

  • Responsible manufacturers want higher standards - 75% call for specific product and manufacturing standards, with two-thirds demanding standardized testing and dosing protocols
  • Education and transparency benefit everyone - 85.7% of those emphasizing education see consumer resources as essential for safe use
  • Community self-regulation is already beginning - Equal numbers (50%) support both industry self-regulation and third-party certification as parallel paths forward
  • Legal frameworks would enable better safety measures - While 37.5% see legalization as necessary for full accountability, the industry isn't waiting

Case Study: PsiloSafe

The accountability systems manufacturers envision are already taking shape. PsiloSafe, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit partnered with Tryptonomics testing laboratories, demonstrates how industry-led standards work in practice.

Their certification system directly implements what manufacturers called for: laboratory testing of every product for psilocybin, psilocin, and other alkaloids; QR codes on packaging linking to data sheets with exact potency levels and dosing guidance; and third-party verification that maintains confidentiality for brands operating in legal gray areas.

Currently working with 15 brands including Counter Culture, Empathacary, Stem Social, and BrainSpa, PsiloSafe addresses the authenticity crisis head-on. As founder Joe Robinson notes, "We want to launch this underground, gray market in a way that is ethically, morally proper and has some risk mitigation initiatives to make sure that a black swan event doesn't pop up and set us back another 20 years."

Laying the Groundwork

By establishing testing protocols, labeling standards, and verification systems now, the underground market is building the foundation for responsible regulation. The industry isn't waiting for permission to do the right thing—safety improvements are happening now, led by those who understand both the profound potential and serious risks of these compounds.
